# Idea Judgment

Covers the internal discipline of evaluating your own ideas honestly — recognizing
when something isn't working, auditing your assumptions, and distinguishing genuine
depth from novelty. Read this when stress-testing a design before converging, or
when you suspect you're defending an idea rather than examining it.

Synthesized from Jonathan Blow stream clips (youtube.com/@JBH-p5b); per-topic sources in docs/sources/jonathan-blow.md.

---

**Judgment/taste is the engine.** You must hold your own opinion of what's good.
Skill is the gap between that opinion and what you can currently produce, and you
close it deliberately. Without an opinion about what's good, you only make good
things by accident. Jonathan Blow argues that cultural relativism — "everything is
equally good if you like it" — is a trap that prevents improvement, because it
removes the standard you'd need to measure progress against.

**Call your own bluff.** Honestly admitting "this kind of sucks" about your own
prototype is a trainable discipline that beginners lack. Treat a prototype as a
navigation tool toward the good version, not a defense of your original vision.
The prototype's job is to reveal what the real thing should be.

**Name then audit your beliefs.** Record your assumptions going in; afterward check
which actually held. Doing this consistently — not just once — is how you improve
fast. Assumptions you never wrote down can't be audited.

**Novelty is not sustained value.** A striking idea's first-reveal "wow" isn't the
same as sustained engagement. Explore the idea for real depth and bring the good
discoveries back — or cut it. Don't abandon the core idea mid-project for a shinier
one; that's novelty addiction, not creative judgment.

**Gather references broad-first.** Collect references with a deliberately low bar
and pull from outside the domain. Early ideation is allowed to be vague — don't
force premature precision. The goal is to expand the space before narrowing it.

**Triage over rabbit-holing.** Recursive rabbit-holing destroys momentum. Reprioritize:
defer a deep problem to ship something testable. The exception is when things are
fundamentally broken — patching a broken foundation leaves you "patching holes in a
boat," and you should go to the root instead.

**Keep your agency.** Defeatist stories — "you can't deliberately make something
good" or "popularity is random" — remove agency. Jonathan Blow argues you can aim
to make something good on purpose, and that framing matters: it keeps you in the
driver's seat rather than waiting for luck.
