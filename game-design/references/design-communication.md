# Design Communication

Making game concepts clear and compelling — how to identify and communicate the hook, what makes a pitch succeed or fail, and how to think about your game from the outside in. Load this when pitching a game concept, evaluating whether a design has a clear identity, or diagnosing why a game is hard to explain.

---

## The Two Questions That Matter

**Source:** Brian Upton, "30 Things I Hate About Your Game Pitch" (GDC 2014).

Every pitch — and every game concept — must answer two questions:

1. **Is this game worth making?** (Does it have a hook? Is it unique? Does it serve a real player need?)
2. **Can this team make it?** (Do they understand scope? Have they mitigated the risks? Are they credible?)

Anything in a pitch that doesn't address one of these questions is noise. This applies equally to design documents, one-pagers, and internal concept reviews.

---

## The Hook

The hook is the unique, specific thing that makes your game different from everything else that already exists. It answers: "Why would someone buy this instead of what's already out there?"

**Hooks are not:**
- Pillars ("strong fantasy story, detailed world, hand-to-hand combat") — these keep you on track during development but don't differentiate
- Genre descriptions ("it's a platformer") — this describes what it is, not why it matters
- Production values ("beautiful graphics, great music") — these are expected, not differentiating

**Hooks are:**
- A specific mechanic that creates a new type of decision
- A unique combination of existing elements that produces unexpected depth
- A perspective or constraint that changes what the game is fundamentally about

*Example:* Rainbow Six's hook wasn't "first-person shooter." It was "what if twitch reflexes didn't matter — what if it was all patience and tactics?" That constraint produced a new type of game.

**Finding the hook:** Take something established and push it off-axis. What happens if you change one core assumption? Iterate on that direction until you find something that produces genuine surprise.

## Moment-to-Moment Clarity

You must be able to describe what the player is actually doing — not the world, not the story, not the systems, but the physical experience of holding the controller and playing.

If you can't describe the moment-to-moment experience clearly, you don't know what your game is yet. This is the most common failure mode: pitches that describe everything except what playing feels like.

**The test:** Can you walk someone through 60 seconds of gameplay? What does the player see, decide, and do? What feedback do they get? What do they want to do next?

## Common Concept Pitfalls

### Backstory Overload
Twenty minutes of lore will not sell your game. Players don't need to know the political history of your fantasy continent to decide if they want to play. If your game can't stand without a lore dump, the concept isn't strong enough.

*Rule of thumb:* How does the original Star Wars begin? A quick text crawl, then action. Set up the situation in seconds, then demonstrate the game.

### Standard Features as Differentiators
Don't explain inventory systems, save mechanics, or other standard features as if they're unique. If it's standard, say "we have an inventory system" and move on. Only explain features that are genuinely different and central to the hook.

### Realism as Justification
"In the real world, you can't double jump" is not a design justification. Almost everything in games is unrealistic. Justify design decisions with design reasons (what experience does this create?) not appeals to realism.

### Undisclosed Tech Risk
If your concept requires something technically ambitious or unproven, say so — and explain how you'll address it. Pitching a 1000-player real-time multiplayer game without acknowledging the technical challenge signals that you don't understand what you're proposing.

### Proof of Concept That Proves Nothing
Prototypes should demonstrate the hook and mitigate the risks. A prototype showing a character walking around proves nothing — that's easy. Prototype the unique mechanic. Prototype the thing that might not work.

### Monetization as the Pitch
Nobody buys a game for its monetization model. Mention your approach briefly (free-to-play, premium, subscription), then move on. Detailed monetization plans belong in development, not in a concept pitch.

### Unknown Scope
If you don't know how big your game is — how many hours, levels, characters, lines of dialogue — you can't assess feasibility. The person evaluating your pitch is running a cost calculation in their head. Give them the numbers.

## Communicating Design Clarity

A well-designed game should be easy to explain. If you can't explain what your game is about in a sentence or two, that's a design problem, not a communication problem.

**The one-sentence test:** What is your game about? Not the story — the mechanic. What does the player do, and why is that interesting?

*Examples:*
- "It's a shooter where standing still gets you killed and aggression restores your health" (DOOM)
- "It's a co-op game where the zombies are designed to punish players who don't stick together" (Left 4 Dead)
- "It's a tower defense game where your units are plants, so players never expect them to move" (Plants vs. Zombies)

Each of these describes the mechanic and why it's interesting in one sentence. If you can't do this, the hook isn't clear yet.

## Pitching as Design Diagnosis

The discipline of pitching forces design clarity. If you can't explain your game compellingly, you probably don't understand it well enough yet. Common signs:

- **You need 20 minutes of backstory** → The game's identity is in the lore, not the mechanics
- **You can't describe moment-to-moment play** → You don't know what the game is yet
- **Your hook is a list of features** → You haven't found the differentiating idea
- **You're asking the audience what they want** → You don't have a creative vision
- **Your prototype shows the easy stuff** → You haven't tested the risky stuff

## Diagnostic Questions

**Hook clarity:**
- Can you state what makes your game unique in one sentence?
- Why would someone buy this instead of what already exists?
- Is your hook a mechanic, a constraint, or a combination — or just a genre description?

**Moment-to-moment clarity:**
- Can you walk someone through 60 seconds of gameplay? What does the player see, decide, and do?
- What does the player want to do next after their first interaction?

**Concept readiness:**
- Do you know your scope? (Hours of gameplay, number of levels, lines of dialogue)
- Have you prototyped the risky, unique parts — not just the easy parts?
- Are you honest about the technical and design risks?

**Design identity:**
- If you removed the theme, would the game still be interesting?
- What is your game mechanically about? Is that the same as what you say it's about?
