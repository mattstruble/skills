# Reactive Dialogue and Narrative Coherence

Covers two interlocking craft concerns: how to write reactive dialogue lines that earn their place (the reactive-dialogue rule), and how incoherent or hand-wavy world details undermine audience investment (coherence as a buy-in mechanism). Read this when writing moment-to-moment dialogue that responds to recent events, when trimming dialogue that feels redundant or out-of-character, or when your world's details feel thin and players aren't investing in the fiction.

Synthesized from Jonathan Blow stream clips (youtube.com/@JBH-p5b); per-topic sources in docs/sources/jonathan-blow.md.

---

## Reactive Dialogue

### The Reactive-Dialogue Rule

A "that just happened" line — dialogue triggered by a recent event — must do one of two things: reveal something new about the character, the situation, or the world, or it must be cut. A line that merely confirms what the player already knows ("We just defeated the guard!") adds no information and trains the player to skip dialogue. The bar is not "does this line make sense here?" but "does this line earn its place?"

Jonathan Blow argues that the most common failure mode is the confirmation line: dialogue that restates the event rather than reacting to it from inside the character's psychology. The character should respond as *themselves*, not as a narrator summarizing the plot.

**Corollary — no out-of-character lines**: A reactive line that is tonally inconsistent with the character's established voice is worse than silence. It breaks the fiction twice: once by adding no information, and again by reminding the player that a writer put words in a character's mouth. If the line doesn't sound like something this specific person would say in this specific moment, cut it.

**Corollary — keep lines short**: Reactive dialogue is not the place for exposition. The event just happened; the player is still processing it. A short, sharp reaction lands harder than a paragraph. Length is a signal of insecurity — the writer explaining what the player should feel rather than trusting the character to convey it.

### The Drafting Process

Jonathan Blow's recommended two-pass approach:

1. **Draft for pacing**: Write the reactive line quickly, prioritizing rhythm and timing. Get something in the scene that doesn't break the flow. Don't edit yet.
2. **Review for character-truth and value**: Return to the line with two questions — (a) does this sound like this character, and (b) does this line reveal something the player didn't already know? If the answer to either is no, cut or rewrite.

The two passes serve different cognitive modes. Pacing is felt in real time; character-truth and value require stepping back. Trying to optimize both simultaneously produces lines that are neither well-timed nor revealing.

## Coherence and Detail

### Coherence as Buy-In Mechanism

Jonathan Blow argues that incoherent details — world-building that doesn't hold together under scrutiny, lore that contradicts itself, mechanics that the fiction can't explain — actively prevent audience investment. The audience's willingness to suspend disbelief is not unlimited; it is a budget. Incoherent details spend that budget without returning anything.

The failure mode is asking the audience to "switch their brain off" — to accept that the details don't quite add up and just enjoy the ride. This caps your ceiling. Players who are willing to think will disengage; players who aren't willing to think will engage shallowly. The game can only reach the depth of engagement it earns through coherence.

**Coherence is not completeness**: You don't need to explain everything. Gaps and mysteries are fine — they engage the player's imagination (see `references/player-imagination.md`). What coherence requires is that the details you *do* show hold together. A world with deliberate mysteries is coherent; a world with accidental contradictions is not.

### Earning Investment Through Detail

Details earn investment when they are:

- **Consistent** with each other and with the world's established rules
- **Specific** enough to feel real rather than generic
- **Purposeful** — they reveal something about the world, a character, or a theme

Generic details (the guard patrols a corridor; the tavern serves ale) are not incoherent, but they don't earn investment either. Specific, consistent details that reward attention — a guard who hums a folk song from a region the player has visited, a tavern that serves a drink only made in this city — signal that the world is real and that paying attention is worthwhile.

Jonathan Blow's position: the ceiling of your game's emotional and intellectual impact is set by the coherence of its details. A game with incoherent details can still be fun, but it cannot be *meaningful* in the way that a coherent world can be.

## Design Checklist

**Reactive dialogue**:
1. Does this line reveal something new — about character, situation, or world?
2. Does this line sound like this specific character in this specific moment?
3. Is this line as short as it can be while still landing?
4. Was it drafted for pacing first, then reviewed for character-truth and value?

**Coherence**:
1. Do the world's details hold together under scrutiny?
2. Are you asking the player to "switch their brain off" anywhere?
3. Are the details you show specific and purposeful, not just generic set-dressing?
4. Do gaps in the world feel like deliberate mysteries or accidental contradictions?
